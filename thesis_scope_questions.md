# Thesis Scope Questions

This document structures the decision space for the scoping audit. It does not verify datasets, recommend a design, or answer the questions deeply yet.

## 1. Core thesis topic candidates

- Which precise thesis topic within regional health inequality and unmet healthcare needs in Europe is feasible with official data?
- Should the thesis focus on unmet healthcare needs broadly, reason-specific unmet needs, socioeconomic inequality in unmet needs, or geographic inequality?
- Should the thesis be framed as European comparative analysis, regional inequality analysis, or Italy-focused extension after a European baseline?

## 2. Main outcome candidates

- Which Eurostat unmet healthcare need indicator can be verified as the safest primary outcome?
- Are overall self-reported unmet needs and reason-specific unmet needs available in compatible forms?
- What is the exact denominator and population definition for each candidate outcome?
- Are the outcomes aggregate published indicators or outputs derived from microdata?

## 3. Geography options

- Is the analysis feasible at national level only?
- Is NUTS2 regional analysis available and sufficiently complete?
- Are all EU countries covered at the same geographic level?
- Are there geography breaks, missing regions, or boundary changes that affect comparability?

## 4. Time coverage options

- Which years are available for each candidate outcome?
- Is a balanced panel feasible?
- Is an unbalanced panel feasible?
- Are there methodological breaks that restrict the safe time period?
- Is a latest-year cross-section more defensible than a panel?

## 5. Aggregate vs microdata decision

- Can the thesis be completed reproducibly using Eurostat aggregate open data?
- Would EU-SILC microdata access materially improve the research question?
- Is microdata access realistic within the Master's thesis timeline?
- What restrictions, permissions, or delays would microdata introduce?

## 6. Candidate explanatory variables from Eurostat

- Which Eurostat variables could serve as conservative regional or national context indicators?
- Are socioeconomic, demographic, health-system, labor-market, or urbanization indicators available at the same geography and years as the outcome?
- Are candidate explanatory variables conceptually aligned with unmet healthcare needs?
- Are definitions and denominators compatible across countries and years?

## 7. Optional ISTAT extension

- Is an Italy-focused ISTAT extension necessary or useful after the Eurostat feasibility audit?
- Does ISTAT provide more granular or more recent data relevant to unmet healthcare needs?
- Would an ISTAT extension improve the thesis or create comparability problems?
- Should ISTAT be limited to a robustness, descriptive, or appendix role?

## 8. Risks that could invalidate the design

- The desired outcome may not be available at NUTS2 level.
- Regional data may exist but be too sparse for a panel.
- Indicator definitions may change over time.
- Published aggregate tables may not contain the needed subgroup or reason breakdowns.
- Candidate controls may not match the outcome geography or years.
- Microdata access may be infeasible within the project timeline.
- Mixing EU-SILC, EHIS, Eurostat aggregate tables, and ISTAT sources may create non-comparable measures.
- Missing data may be systematic rather than random.

## 9. Questions that must be answered before Phase 1 thesis design

- What is the verified primary Eurostat dataset code and title?
- What is the verified outcome definition?
- What is the verified denominator?
- What are the verified dimensions and filters?
- What years are verified as available?
- What geographic level is verified as available?
- Is regional panel construction actually feasible?
- Are there verified methodological breaks or comparability warnings?
- Which design is recommended, which is possible but risky, and which is not recommended?
- What evidence should be shown to the professor before committing to the thesis design?
