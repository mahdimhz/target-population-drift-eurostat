\section{Estimand Framework}

This chapter is the empirical core of the thesis. It treats the poverty or social-exclusion coefficient as a stress test for estimand drift: the coefficient is followed as the model moves from the intended outcome-observed monitoring panel to selected complete cases, population-weighted complete cases, universe-restricted panels, IPW diagnostics, MAR imputation, and MNAR stress tests. The question is not whether one selected-panel coefficient is statistically marked. The question is whether the aggregate poverty association is stable when the target population, weighting rule, row-selection process, and missing-data assumption change.

The hierarchy is estimand-first. Complete-case and population-weighted complete-case estimates use the same selected rows but answer different country-versus-resident monitoring questions. Balanced-outcome means balanced for the primary outcome and valid population weights, not covariate-balanced. IPW is a selection diagnostic among observed complete cases. MAR and MNAR MI rows target the broader outcome-observed panel under explicit model-based assumptions and are not treated as corrected estimates.

The matrix changes the interpretation of the empirical evidence. Long population-denominator indicators can support several observed-data estimands. Need-denominator indicators are useful for monitoring, but their observed complete-case FE rows are too short for the deterministic support rule and are therefore non-identifiable as complete-case regression targets. Table~\ref{tab:outcome_failure_classification} applies the deterministic labels defined in the introduction. The classifications are deliberately conservative. The labels are used to discipline interpretation: they prevent the thesis from turning a selected-panel coefficient into a broad claim about European health access.

\section{Semi-Synthetic Missingness Simulation}

The real Eurostat panel cannot identify a single reference coefficient without additional assumptions. To test the audit logic under controlled conditions, the thesis adds a semi-synthetic missingness simulation. The simulation starts from the most complete primary-outcome reference panel: the 282 country-years used in the baseline complete-case FE model. The reference estimand is the poverty/social-exclusion coefficient estimated on this complete artificial panel. Covariate values are then removed under six mechanisms: MCAR row-level missingness, early-year missingness, country-group missingness, financing-block missingness, MNAR high-poverty/high-unmet missingness, and Eurostat-realistic missingness based on observed source coverage.

The term \emph{reference estimand} is used deliberately. It is not a claim that the 282-row panel reveals a definitive European coefficient. It is a controlled benchmark for measuring how complete-case FE, population-weighted FE, IPW FE, PMM MI, and MNAR-shift MI behave when the complete artificial panel is known before deleting covariates.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\scriptsize
\caption{Semi-synthetic missingness simulation summary. The reference estimand is the poverty/social-exclusion coefficient from the complete 282-row primary panel. Each mechanism uses 100 replicates; the table reports selected estimators to show bias, sign reversal, interval coverage of the reference estimand, and wrong-conclusion rates.}
\label{tab:simulation_main_summary}
\input{tables/simulation_main_summary}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:simulation_main_summary} shows that the direction of failure depends on the missingness mechanism. Complete-case FE is relatively stable under country-group and covariate-block deletion in this reference panel, but it shows sign reversals under MCAR deletion because the retained rows become small and noisy. PMM MI and MNAR-shift MI are not uniformly better: under early-year and country-group deletion, they can move far from the reference estimand. This is the simulation analogue of the thesis's main empirical caution: missing-data methods change assumptions and target populations; they do not automatically repair public-data non-coverage.

The simulation therefore validates the reporting logic rather than introducing a new estimator: public aggregate-panel studies should report target definition, row loss, selection distortion, estimator sensitivity, and non-identifiability before interpreting substantive coefficients.

\section{Selected Complete-Case Baseline}

The pooled specification relates unmet medical care need in country \(i\) and year \(t\) to country-year covariates and year indicators:
\[
y_{it} = \alpha + X_{it}\beta + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}.
\]
The main fixed-effects specification adds country indicators:
\[
y_{it} = \alpha_i + X_{it}\beta + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}.
\]
Both pooled and two-way fixed-effects models report country-clustered standard errors with a finite-sample correction.

\section{IPW Diagnostic}

IPW is retained as a diagnostic, not as a fix for structural Eurostat non-coverage. The weights are estimated from observed unmet need, year, country group, macro conditions, missingness indicators, and coverage flags, then stabilized and truncated. In this dataset IPW changes little because the complete-case sample is selected mainly through structural covariate availability. Reweighting observed complete cases cannot recover rows where required covariates are absent.

\section{Interpretation}

The regression conclusion is estimand dependence, not a stable poverty effect. The selected complete-case model gives a substantively plausible positive association, and several observed covariate-complete perturbations point in the same direction. But the coefficient changes with model structure, weighting, measurement-period restrictions, country trends, and especially the move from selected complete cases to model-based outcome-observed targets.

This is the thesis spine. Complete-case modelling in open Eurostat health-monitoring panels can silently move the analyst from an intended European monitoring population to a selected covariate-complete panel. The poverty coefficient is useful because it reveals that drift; it is not presented as a durable causal, policy, or individual-level finding.
