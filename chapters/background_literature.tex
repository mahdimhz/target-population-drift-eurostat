\section{Healthcare Access Theory}

Unmet medical care is an access indicator rather than a direct measure of service use or clinical need. Andersen's behavioural model and the Aday-Andersen framework relate realised care to predisposing characteristics, enabling resources, need, and health-system organisation \citep{aday_andersen_1974_framework,andersen_1995_behavioral_model}. Penchansky and Thomas describe access through availability, accessibility, affordability, accommodation, and acceptability \citep{penchansky_thomas_1981_access}. Levesque, Harris, and Russell further conceptualise access as an interaction between health-system characteristics and people's abilities to perceive, seek, reach, pay for, and engage with care \citep{levesque_harris_russell_2013_access}.

Together, these frameworks show why unmet care may arise through several mechanisms. Financial constraints relate to affordability; physicians and hospital beds provide coarse indicators of availability; and macroeconomic resources and labour-market conditions shape the context in which access is sought. In the country-year panel, poverty or social exclusion and out-of-pocket spending represent affordability pressures, GDP per capita represents national resources, unemployment represents labour-market vulnerability, and country and year effects represent persistent institutional differences and common period shocks. This mapping organises the aggregate indicators without treating them as direct measures of individual access pathways.

\section{Measurement of Self-Reported Unmet Care}

Eurostat's EU-SILC health component contains self-reported health-status and unmet-needs variables for medical and dental care \citep{eurostat_health_variables_metadata}. Respondents assess whether they needed an examination or treatment but did not receive or seek it; medical care excludes dental care and concerns treatment provided by, or under the supervision of, medical professionals \citep{eurostat_health_variables_silc_methodology}. The listed reasons include expense, waiting lists, work or care responsibilities, distance or transport, fear, waiting for improvement, difficulty identifying a provider, and other reasons. The thesis focuses on the aggregate access-barrier category combining expense, distance, and waiting lists, while retaining the separate reasons in the monitoring benchmark.

EU-SILC operates within a common legislative and methodological framework intended to support comparison across participating countries \citep{eurostat_eusilc_methodology}. Implementation nevertheless combines interviews, national survey procedures, administrative sources, and imputation, making explicit documentation of coverage and definitions necessary \citep{eurostat_silc_information_data}. The target population is also bounded: EU-SILC covers private households, interviews people aged 16 or older for the health variables, and generally excludes collective households and institutions \citep{eurostat_silc_information_data,eurostat_income_living_metadata}. The resulting indicators measure reported need and reported non-receipt of care, not administrative denial or an objective measure of health-system quality.

Denominator choice is central to interpretation. The primary indicator, \texttt{hlth\_silc\_08}, reports unmet medical examination or treatment among the population aged 16 or older. The need-denominator family, including \texttt{hlth\_silc\_08b}, reports unmet access among people who first reported a medical need \citep{eurostat_unmet_needs_statistics,eurostat_health_variables_metadata}. A population-denominator rate therefore combines the reporting of need with the non-receipt of care, whereas a need-denominator rate conditions on reported need. These indicators answer different monitoring questions and are not interchangeable.

This distinction became more explicit after changes to the EU-SILC health-needs questionnaire. \citet{A4_ramos_2019} show why conventional unmet-need measures can obscure the distinction between reporting need and obtaining care, while \citet{ingleby_guidi_2025_unmet_needs_methodology} explain that the questionnaire design introduced from 2015 separates these stages more clearly. Because the baseline complete-case regression begins in 2015, the thesis cannot support a well-powered pre/post-2015 coefficient comparison. It instead reports an excluding-2015 check, a 2016-onward restriction, and descriptive comparisons where the underlying indicators permit them.

\section{European Evidence on Social Gradients}

Individual-level European studies consistently associate unmet care with social and economic vulnerability. Lower income, unemployment, difficulty making ends meet, poor health, debt, and housing vulnerability are linked to higher reported access barriers in EU-SILC and related survey evidence \citep{A1_chaupain_guillot_2015,A2_madureira_lima_2018,A3_israel_2016,A5_cylus_papanicolas_2015}. Research on financing and social protection further connects out-of-pocket payments, policy design, and public pensions with cost-related unmet need \citep{B2_kaminska_wulfgramm_2019,C4_reeves_mckee_mackenbach_2017}. Studies of Greece, Portugal, and the Baltic states show how economic crises and health-system pressure can coincide with worsening access \citep{zavras_zavras_kyriopoulos_2016,legido_quigley_karanikolos_2016,karanikolos_gordeev_mackenbach_2016}.

This evidence supports poverty or social exclusion as the principal stress-test covariate: affordability and material insecurity are recurrent explanations for unmet care. The evidence also establishes the boundary of the comparison. Microdata studies observe individual or household circumstances and can adjust for health status, income, employment, education, and other personal characteristics. The present study observes national summaries. Its question is therefore whether an aggregate social-vulnerability association survives changes in the monitored and analytical population, not whether it reproduces an individual-level gradient.

Citizenship differences are treated similarly. Migration status, legal entitlement, language, and socioeconomic position may jointly shape access, but aggregate citizenship rows cannot separate those mechanisms. They provide descriptive equity context and remain secondary to the target-population audit.

\section{Aggregate Monitoring and Ecological Inference}

Country-year panels are valuable for monitoring cross-national trends and institutional context, but aggregation changes what an association can mean. Classic work demonstrates that ecological correlations may differ from individual-level relationships \citep{robinson_1950}. Epidemiological research identifies additional sources of ecological bias, including compositional differences, contextual confounding, aggregation, and effect modification \citep{greenland_morgenstern_1989}.

Accordingly, a positive country-year poverty coefficient may reflect changes in population composition, national policy, reporting expectations, macroeconomic shocks, health-system pressure, or data coverage. It does not by itself establish that poorer individuals were denied care. The appropriate object is an aggregate monitoring estimand: a coefficient defined by an outcome, denominator, country universe, time window, weighting rule, row set, and missing-data assumption. This framing makes country-year analysis useful for the thesis's question while keeping individual mechanisms outside its empirical scope.

\section{Missing Data, Complete Cases, and Estimand Drift}

Missing covariates create the central analytical problem because the outcome is observed for more country-years than the baseline regression variables. Complete-case analysis may consequently change both sample size and target population. Standard missing-data theory distinguishes complete-case estimation, inverse-probability weighting, and multiple imputation through their assumptions about observation and missingness \citep{rubin_1987,little_rubin_2019}. IPW depends on an adequate observation model and positivity; it cannot reconstruct covariates that are structurally unavailable \citep{seaman_white_2013}.

The empirical concern is therefore not only loss of precision. If excluded country-years differ in unmet care, poverty, GDP, unemployment, period, or institutional group, the complete-case coefficient describes a selected panel. Comparing included and excluded rows, documenting sequential attrition, and reporting country and year retention are direct diagnostics of this change in target.

Multiple imputation supplies a model-based sensitivity analysis for the broader outcome-observed panel. Its interpretation depends on a MAR-type assumption that missing covariates can be represented using observed outcomes, covariates, country structure, and time structure. Structural Eurostat non-coverage and variables available only for restricted periods may violate that assumption. For this reason, the thesis compares imputation specifications and applies MNAR delta shifts to originally missing values. Disagreement across complete-case, IPW, MAR, and MNAR analyses is interpreted as evidence of estimand or model dependence rather than as a contest for one corrected coefficient.

\section{Panel Inference, Validation, and Reporting Discipline}

Repeated country observations require dependence-aware uncertainty. Country-clustered standard errors permit within-country error correlation in fixed-effects models \citep{arellano_1987}, while serial correlation can otherwise produce overconfident inference \citep{bertrand_duflo_mullainathan_2004}. The main selected-panel coefficient is therefore reported with country-clustered uncertainty and compared with Driscoll--Kraay and wild-cluster bootstrap diagnostics, following panel-dependence and clustered-inference references \citep{driscoll_kraay_1998,cameron_gelbach_miller_2008}.

The prediction appendix follows the same concern with dependence and target definition. Random row splits can overstate generalisation when observations are temporal or grouped, so validation uses temporal and country-blocked designs \citep{roberts_2017_cross_validation}. TRIPOD+AI and PROBAST+AI similarly require transparent definition of the target population, outcome, predictors, validation design, uncertainty, and risk of bias \citep{collins_moons_dhiman_2024_tripod_ai,moons_damen_kaul_2025_probast_ai}. These principles discipline the one-year-ahead benchmark; they do not turn it into a separate prediction contribution.

The broader thesis follows a STROBE-style reporting logic by documenting data sources, eligibility, variables, bias, statistical methods, missing data, and limitations \citep{von_elm_altman_egger_2007_strobe}. Each reported coefficient is therefore accompanied by its row set, countries, years, fixed effects, weighting, covariance treatment, and missing-data assumption. These features are part of the estimand rather than ancillary technical details.

\section{Research Gap and Analytical Response}

The literature establishes that unmet care is multidimensional and self-reported, that social vulnerability is associated with access barriers, that aggregate relationships cannot identify individual mechanisms, and that incomplete data require explicit assumptions. What remains less developed is a reproducible procedure for showing how these issues interact when public Eurostat indicators are converted into country-year regression panels. Studies commonly focus on one outcome definition or one analytical sample without treating the movement between monitoring and regression populations as the principal empirical object.

This thesis addresses that gap by registering each outcome and target population before modelling, benchmarking alternative denominators and weighting rules, measuring complete-case attrition and selection distortion, and comparing coefficients across observed-data and imputed estimands. A semi-synthetic simulation then evaluates how estimators behave when Eurostat-style missingness is imposed after a reference estimand has been defined. Chapters~\ref{ch:data_indicators}--\ref{ch:modeling_strategy_results} implement this sequence, converting the literature's measurement and missing-data concerns into a reproducible target-population drift audit.
