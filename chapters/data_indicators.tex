\section{Data Source and Unit of Analysis}

The thesis uses published aggregate Eurostat data, with the country-year as the unit of analysis. Each row is one national observation in one calendar year. Unlike EU-SILC microdata, the panel does not contain linked individual or household records; it is designed for reproducible national monitoring and aggregate association analysis \citep{eurostat_eusilc_methodology,eurostat_silc_information_data}.

The analytical files retain national units and exclude EU and euro-area aggregates. Regional NUTS2 dataflows were audited but are not used because a consistent regional panel was not verified. All outcomes and covariates are merged by country and year using documented dataset codes and filters; the appendix source register records extraction dates and source definitions.

The first generated output is the public-data estimand registry in Table~\ref{tab:public_data_estimand_registry}. It converts official Eurostat fields into explicit analytical objects by recording the care type, barrier, denominator, coverage, and feasibility of each indicator before modelling. Medical and dental outcomes, population- and need-denominator definitions, and medical cost, distance, and waiting-list barriers are included. Reason codes are validated against downloaded JSON-stat metadata; an indicator that fails validation remains registered with an infeasibility reason.

\section{Primary Outcome}

The primary outcome is drawn from \texttt{hlth\_silc\_08}, Eurostat's EU-SILC indicator for self-reported unmet need for medical examination by sex, age, main reason, and income quantile \citep{eurostat_health_variables_metadata,eurostat_unmet_needs_statistics}. The extraction selects total sex, age 16 and over, total income quantile, percentage units, and the combined expense, distance, or waiting-list reason. The derived analytical field is \texttt{unmet\_need\_pc}.

The indicator measures a reported access barrier, not clinical need or health-system quality. Because it uses the population denominator, its national rate combines the reporting of need with the non-receipt of care; the shorter \texttt{hlth\_silc\_08b} series conditions on persons reporting the same medical needs. This denominator distinction is part of the estimand registry rather than a claim that either measure is universally preferable \citep{guidi_ingleby_2023_valid_data,ingleby_guidi_2025_unmet_needs_methodology}.

\section{Covariate Blocks}

Table~\ref{tab:variable_summary} separates official Eurostat indicators from author-engineered features and groups them into macroeconomic, labour-market, inequality, health-financing, and health-capacity blocks. The blocks operationalise the access concepts reviewed in Chapter~\ref{ch:background_literature}; they do not measure individual mechanisms.

GDP per capita and unemployment describe macroeconomic and labour-market context. Poverty or social exclusion is the principal social-vulnerability stress-test variable, while the Gini coefficient and long-term unemployment provide supporting distributional context. Government expenditure, compulsory financing, and out-of-pocket spending represent national financing arrangements; physicians and hospital beds are coarse capacity measures. These national indicators cannot resolve local distribution, specialty shortages, prices, or individual resources, so their role is to define comparable country-year adjustment blocks.

\section{Engineered Features}

The pipeline creates logged GDP, annual within-country changes, and one-year lags. These deterministic transformations are author-generated fields derived only after sorting by country and year. No values are carried forward: lag and change construction therefore creates explicit additional missingness at series boundaries, which is retained in the sample audit.

\section{Population Weights and Target Populations}

Population weights use Eurostat \texttt{demo\_pjan}, filtered to annual frequency, number of persons, total age, and total sex. The outcome-observed panel covers 2008--2025; the baseline complete-case FE sample covers 2015--2024 because the required covariates begin later and remain incomplete in 2025.

The unweighted descriptive estimand is an average-country-year quantity over the observed outcome set \(O\):
\[
\mu_{O} = \frac{1}{|O|}\sum_{(i,t)\in O} y_{it},
\]
where \(y_{it}\) is unmet medical care need in country \(i\) and year \(t\). This estimand treats each observed country-year equally.

Population-weighted estimates use country-year populations normalized within each year:
\[
w_{it} = \frac{N_{it}}{\sum_{j \in O_t} N_{jt}},
\]
where \(N_{it}\) is country \(i\)'s population in year \(t\), and \(O_t\) is the set of observed countries in that year. Each year therefore contributes equally, while larger countries receive greater weight within year. This defines an average-European-resident monitoring estimand.

For a regression coefficient, the complete-case estimand is conditional on the observed covariate-complete set \(C\):
\[
\beta_{CC} \text{ is estimated from } \{(i,t): y_{it},X_{it}\ \text{are observed for all baseline covariates}\}.
\]
The population-weighted complete-case estimand uses the same set \(C\) but applies \(w_{it}\). The MI full-target estimand keeps the broader outcome-observed set \(O\) and replaces missing covariates with draws from the imputation model:
\[
\beta_{MI} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\hat{\beta}^{(m)}_{O},
\]
with uncertainty pooled using Rubin's rules. This notation is a reminder that MI changes the target and adds model dependence; it does not establish a target-invariant coefficient.

The planned MNAR sensitivity estimand keeps the same outcome-observed set \(O\) but applies explicit departures from the MAR imputation model:
\[
\beta_{MNAR(\delta)} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\hat{\beta}^{(m)}_{O,\delta},
\]
where \(\delta\) denotes a documented shift to imputed values for missing poverty, GDP, or unemployment. This estimand is a stress test for imputation assumptions, not a corrected estimate.

Population weighting is not treated as more correct than unweighted analysis. The unweighted panel answers an average-country monitoring question, while the weighted panel answers an average-resident monitoring question. The balanced-outcome panel is also defined at this stage: it includes countries with complete primary-outcome coverage and valid population weights over the main analysis window. It is not automatically balanced for every regression covariate.

\section{Missingness and Sample Construction}

Covariate coverage is shorter and less complete than outcome coverage. The baseline two-way FE model therefore retains \(N=282\) country-years with all selected covariates, rather than all 608 outcome-observed rows. Chapter~\ref{ch:descriptive_evidence} treats that movement as an empirical result through the attrition waterfall, grouped missingness heatmaps, and included-versus-excluded comparisons.

The 2015 marker used in descriptive figures is not treated as a formal regression discontinuity. Because the baseline complete-case regression starts in 2015, the thesis cannot estimate a valid complete-case pre/post-2015 split for the poverty coefficient. Instead, Chapter~\ref{ch:modeling_strategy_results} reports excluding-2015 and 2016-onward diagnostics, while the descriptive chapter treats pre/post-2015 outcome patterns cautiously.

\section{Scope Exclusions}

EU-SILC microdata, EHIS, ISTAT, and regional NUTS2 series are outside the core panel because they would change the unit, survey context, or verified extraction design. Dental indicators remain separate monitoring outcomes rather than substitutes for the medical-care target. The resulting scope is a national aggregate Eurostat panel centred on \texttt{hlth\_silc\_08}, with \texttt{hlth\_silc\_08b} used for denominator sensitivity.
